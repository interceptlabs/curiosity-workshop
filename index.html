What is this

Reality has drifted. Something shifted and now the world works differently. Your job? Figure out what's different about it, who lives there, and then invent a digital thing its inhabitants desperately need. An app, a game, a tool, a toy — whatever feels right. Everyone throws in ideas, the group riffs until something clicks, and then you actually build a quick prototype together.

How to Play

Round 1

Discover the World

5 minutes

Draw 2 WORLD cards and read them aloud. The group votes on which world they want to explore — or mashes both together into something even more interesting.

This is everyone's shared reality for the rest of the game. Riff on it. What does breakfast look like here? What's on the news?

Round 2

Invent

10 minutes

Draw 1 WHO card and 1 WILD card for the whole group. These are everyone's shared constraints.

Now you need a digital thing — an app, a game, a tool, an experience, an unexpected toy — that your WHO desperately needs in this DRIFT world, following your WILD constraint.

Everyone throws out ideas. Riff on each other's suggestions. Combine things. Argue. The idea doesn't belong to anyone — it belongs to the room.

Round 3

Shape It

10 minutes

By now the group has landed on something — maybe it's two ideas mashed together, maybe someone said something dumb that turned out to be brilliant. Either way, this is where it gets a shape.

Give it a name. Give it a one-line tagline. Sketch it out on a whiteboard or a napkin. Everyone has their two cents — that's the point. The best version of the idea is the one nobody could've come up with alone.

Round 4

Build the Artifact

20+ minutes

The idea the group shaped becomes the build project. Everyone collaborates to create a quick, scrappy digital prototype. This could be a clickable mockup, a tiny web page, a playable mini-game, a different interactive thing — whatever captures the spirit of the idea.

It doesn't need to be polished. It needs to be real enough to show someone and make them go "wait, what is this?"

Example Round

Sample Draw
WORLD: You can't live in the same place for more than 3 years
WHO: Long-distance friends
WILD: It creates something you can send to someone

"Anchor" — a tool for long-distance friends in a world where everyone has to relocate every 3 years. Every time you move, you and your friend each pin one memory to your shared map — a meal you had, a bench you sat on, a dumb photo. Over the years the map fills up with these little anchors across dozens of cities. You can print a physical copy for each other. The friendship has more geography than either person does.

Facilitator Tips

A few things that make this game sing.

Embrace the dumb ideas

The sillier the idea, the more fun the build. Don't optimize for "good" — optimize for "I can't believe we're making this."

Time pressure is your friend

10 minutes to invent forces gut instincts over overthinking. Play music. Keep it moving.

Build ugly, build fast

The artifact doesn't need to be polished. A napkin-sketch prototype that captures the vibe beats a perfect mockup every time.

Remix the decks

Add your own cards. Inside jokes, industry-specific weirdness, things your team is obsessed with. The game gets better when it's yours.

Scale it up or down

3 people works. 12 people works. The conversation just changes shape. Bigger groups get louder and wilder — that's a feature, not a bug.

The build is the point

Rounds 1-3 are the warmup. Round 4 is where it gets real. Give it space. Let people surprise themselves.

Design Lineage

DRIFT borrows shamelessly from the best. The combinatorial card mechanic comes from The Thing From The Future (Situation Lab), which proved that random constraints across multiple categories create millions of surprising prompts. The emphasis on playful ambiguity is borrowed from Dixit, where open interpretation is the whole game. The lateral-thinking curveball (WILD cards) takes a page from Brian Eno's Oblique Strategies. And the "discover, invent, build" arc draws on speculative design workshop practice, where imagining a world and then making artifacts from it is how futures become tangible.

The difference? DRIFT is designed to end with something real. Not just a description or a sketch, but a working (if scrappy) digital thing that didn't exist before the game started.
